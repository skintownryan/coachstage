A practical guide to what young athletes need at every stage of development — and what they're actually ready for.

Stage 1 — Foundation

Ages 5–8  ·  The play years  ·  Building movement literacy

Physical capabilities

Rapid neurological development — this is the best window for learning movement patterns. Coordination, balance, and basic agility are highly trainable. Cardiovascular system responds well but tires quickly and recovers fast. Strength gains are minimal — it's all about patterns.

Balance & coordination Agility foundations Short bursts only Tires quickly
Cognitive capabilities

Short attention span (10–15 min focused activity). Learns best through imitation and play. Cannot hold complex instructions. Egocentric thinking means team concepts are abstract and largely meaningless at this age.

Imitative learning Simple 1–2 step rules No tactical thinking Abstract rules difficult
What they need

Fun above everything else. Wide variety of movement. Positive reinforcement constantly. Small-sided games. No score-keeping. Freedom to explore.

Training focus

Fundamental movement skills: running, jumping, catching, throwing, kicking, balancing. Multi-sport exposure is strongly recommended.

Session structure

20–45 min max. Lots of variety. No long drills. Games-based learning. Frequent water breaks. High praise-to-correction ratio.

Stage 2 — Development

Ages 9–12  ·  The skill window  ·  Peak capacity for technical learning

Physical capabilities

Often called the "skill hungry years." Coordination and motor learning are at their peak. Aerobic fitness responds very well. Strength gains are neural — real improvement without muscle bulk. Girls may begin puberty (10–11), boys later. Growth plates are active — watch for overuse injuries.

Peak skill acquisition Aerobic development Speed development Load management needed
Cognitive capabilities

Logical thinking emerging. Can follow multi-step instructions. Starting to understand rules and team roles. Motivated by skill mastery and peer recognition. Beginning to link cause and effect in performance.

Multi-step instructions Rule understanding Simple tactics emerging Peer approval important
What they need

Technical challenge matched to ability. Increasing complexity as skills grow. Peer connection and belonging. Some competition but fun still primary.

Training focus

Sport-specific technical skills, agility, speed, basic bodyweight strength. Begin introducing simple tactical concepts. Multi-sport still encouraged.

Session structure

60–75 min. Skill repetition with variation. Short team talks (under 5 min). Competition within practice. Specific, constructive feedback.

Stage 3 — Build to Compete

Ages 13–15  ·  The transition years  ·  Navigating puberty and identity

Physical capabilities

Puberty creates huge individual variation. Boys see rapid increases in strength, power, and size. Girls often experience temporary coordination dips as body shape changes rapidly. Both sexes face elevated injury risk — especially ACL (girls) and growth plate stress (boys). Aerobic and anaerobic capacity both highly trainable.

Strength gains possible Power development High injury risk period Huge individual variation
Cognitive capabilities

Abstract reasoning developing. Can engage with tactics and strategy. Strong social and identity motivations — peer acceptance is very influential. May challenge coaches or disengage if they feel unseen. Emotional volatility is normal and expected.

Tactical learning ready Abstract strategy Emotional regulation variable Identity disruption common
What they need

Autonomy and voice. Individual attention. A clear link between effort and outcome. Honest feedback. Psychological safety to make mistakes without shame.

Training focus

Strength and power (weight room appropriate now with good technique). Tactical understanding. Sport-specific conditioning. Injury prevention — hip, knee, ankle stability.

Session structure

75–90 min. Structured warm-up. Mix of individual and team work. Involve athletes in decisions. Meaningful competition exposure.

Stage 4 — Train to Compete

Ages 16–18  ·  Performance years  ·  Approaching adult physical capacity

Physical capabilities

Most athletes approaching adult physical capacity. Strength and power training highly effective. Aerobic and anaerobic systems well developed. Growth plate closure nearing completion (17–18). Can tolerate higher volumes and intensities. Recovery still faster than adults. Gaps between early and late developers now narrow significantly.

Near-adult strength High volume tolerance Full tactical engagement Overtraining risk
Cognitive capabilities

Executive function maturing (prefrontal cortex still developing until ~25). Can self-analyse, set goals, and understand complex game models. Motivated by performance outcomes and future opportunity. Respond well to coach-athlete dialogue. Mental performance skills can be deliberately trained.

Complex tactics Self-regulation Goal setting Mental skills trainable
What they need

Ownership of their development. Honest performance feedback. Mental performance support. Clear pathways forward. Balance between sport and the rest of life.

Training focus

Sport-specific periodisation. Strength and power development. Advanced tactics. Mental skills: focus, confidence, pre-competition routines.

Session structure

90 min+. Periodised weekly loads. Self-directed warm-up. Athlete-led tactical discussion. Regular one-to-ones. Competition as a development tool.

Why this exists

Every weekend, hundreds of thousands of volunteer coaches show up for children who depend on them to make sport a good experience. Most of these coaches are passionate, well-meaning, and completely untrained in child development.

The result? Sessions designed for adult athletes delivered to 8-year-olds. Tactical systems taught to kids who can't yet think tactically. Pressure applied at the exact moment a young athlete needs space to explore. Not from bad intent — from a gap in knowledge.

CoachStage exists to close that gap. Not with academic papers or long textbooks, but with clear, practical information a coach can use before Saturday's session.

Core principles

01

Development before performance

Winning at under-9 level is meaningless. A child who loves movement at age 8 and is still playing at 18 has won. The goal is long-term participation and development, not short-term results.

02

Biological age varies wildly

Two 13-year-olds can have a three-year gap in biological development. Judging athletes against each other during puberty is unfair and often wrong. The late developer may simply be a slow starter.

03

The skill window is real and limited

Between ages 9 and 12, the brain is wired for rapid motor learning in a way it never will be again. Missing this window means playing catch-up later. Invest in technical quality during this period above all else.

04

Fun is not optional at any stage

Enjoyment is the single biggest predictor of long-term participation. An athlete who doesn't enjoy training won't stay long enough to reach their potential. A coach's first job is to make the sport worth coming back to.

05

Coach behaviour shapes more than performance

Young athletes remember how coaches made them feel long after they've forgotten the drills. Psychological safety, belonging, and a sense of progress matter as much as any technical input.

Built on

The framework is drawn from Long-Term Athlete Development (LTAD) research, the work of Istvan Balyi and Richard Way, Sport Canada's LTAD model, the FA's Youth Pathway guidelines, and developmental psychology research on motor learning, cognitive development, and adolescent behaviour.
